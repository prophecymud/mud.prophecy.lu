Approaching An Area

Urgit gives some thoughts on organising building an area.

So, you've sent in your Builder Application, been approved and set up on the Builders' Port, and, if you're new to Building, got some practice creating a few bits pieces in a test area. Now you're ready to start on an area of your own. At this point, while it's exciting, the task ahead can seem frightening. What should I do first? What order should I do things in? How do I turn the ideas I have into a quality complete area?

While there are infinite ways in which you can approach an area, and lots of great areas have been produced for Prophecy using different methods, I thought I'd outline an order of proceeding and some tips that have worked for me, ideas that I hope would help new builders to split up the task into manageable sections, allow you to work steadily towards a great area and avoid wasting time and effort. I'm not suggesting this is a complete or foolproof blueprint, but I hope it might be a decent starting point to finding your own ideal strategy.

1. Choosing an Area

It might seem an obvious step that requires little thought, but you can help yourself by picking a suitable area.

The type of area you pick will be affected by how big you want to it to be. Cities and vast geographical features really requires a large area to do them justice. What you pick may also be affected by what books you're read (having read about an area is an advantage) or the type of area you'd enjoy building (some people prefer wilderness areas, while many others prefer to portray the confines of civilization).

Don't pick something too big if you're new to building. It's much better to complete a small, neat area than to get swamped by the prospect of a huge one and get discouraged and give up. Once you have everything practiced and you know what your strengths are, there's always the possibility of then building something more ambitious.

2. Research

It's too easy to just jump into building at this stage, where a little preparation would save time and problems later on. If you were going to build a house, you'd sit down and think through what you want and what makes a good home, and make some plans, rather than grab the bricks and cement as soon as you've found a plot.

There are many ways in which you can research and prepare:

a) Take a look at suitable parts in the books. If you've read them, it's worth looking over parts where your area or similar areas are mentioned. There may be descriptions you can use as inspiration for your own descriptions later on. Even if your own area is not specifically described, appropriately similar areas probably are. If you're building a Tolnedran city, for example, read through the descriptions of Tol Honeth and it's people.

b) Ask people. If you've not read the books, others may be able to help you locate suitable passages for you to read, or give you some idea what the people of a particular nation are like. The members of the Role Playing Council (RPC) would be glad to help in this regard, since it helps us create more areas that are consistent with the works of Eddings. Other Builders who have built similar areas may also be able to give advice, for example.

c) Search the Web. There are several Eddings sites that might give you ideas, but more general research is possible too. If you're building a forest, for instance, you may well be able to find a site that will give you ideas for types of tree and features to include, making writing descriptions later on considerably easier. By researching thoroughly you give yourself a much easier time if you have problems with ideas and inspiration later on.

3. Make a Map

While you might think you can visualise in detail what your area will look like, making a map really helps in so many ways. It enables you to get the best layout possible- so after drawing it out once you might think of appropriate additions or changes, for example. You see if you have enough vnums for your plan and thus if you need to ask for a few more. It gives you a reference point that you can turn to at any time in the process of building. It also lets you mark off your progress as you go along, so you know what you've done and what you still have to do. (I always have a hard copy of my map to hand as I'm building, where I can mark things as complete, or take notes of where I want to add features later in the building process. When I'm finished, I'd rather have a messy map than a messy area.)

There are several free programs that you can download that will create good maps, including these:

- ASCII Mapmaker
- MapMaker

However, a lot of people (myself included) choose to draw maps by hand on squared paper. It's mainly about finding a method you're comfortable with and that works well for you.

4. Lay Out Rooms

Once I have my map, I should know within a little where I want all my rooms and how I want them connected to one another. Some people think it's best to make one or a few rooms, then a mob, then an object, then a few more rooms, etc. That way allows variety, but I strongly believe it's best to do all of one thing first- so complete rooms before going on to mobs, for example. Doing so allows more consistency throughout the area, and often by doing lots of one thing at a time you become quicker and more efficient at it.

As I dig out the rooms and link them, I tend to mark each room on my map with a dot once each is created, so I can easily see what I've done and still need to do. You can also mark the numbers of key vnums on your map, so you can quickly find and go to them later on.

It's possible to set room names, room flags and sectors as you go along, or go through after all the rooms are created and do it.

As I'm laying out rooms, I also start making a list of mobs that I want to create later on. When I've laid out a Guardhouse, I'd note the need for a guard mobile to put in it. Starting such a list now saves me effort later on, when I might have forgotten some of the mobs I intended to include. I might also now start a list of objects I'll need to create later on- so as I make a temple, for example, I'll note that I want to create an altar object to put in it.

5. Write Room Descriptions

This part takes some time, especially if you want to write lots of good, interesting, varied, detailed descriptions. Lots of people give up areas at this stage, but it's really worth sticking with it, because once this part is done you're a huge step nearer to completing the area. Some people motivate themselves by setting a target of a few descriptions each day or a certain amount each week. Some find it best to work hard on it for a while and then give themselves a few days off to recharge. Whatever you do, don't give up and thus waste your hard work and ideas. Hopefully, if you did a fair amount of research earlier on you'll have lots of relevant ideas to make the task of writing descriptions easier and faster.

Once I've written a description for a room, I draw a line through it on my map, again so I know what I've done. I might also in writing descriptions think of more ideas to add to my mobile/object lists.

6. Create Mobiles

Now we can create all the mobiles our area needs. Hopefully I've built up a good list of ideas while researching and making the rooms, and now I can edit that, add some more to the list and make them, marking them off the list as I do so. While I'm creating mobs, I'll also think of ideas for objects that they might be arrying/using, so I can continue to add them to my list of objects, which will again help me later on. If I create a gardener, I might not down to create a rake for him to carry, for example. All the time we can concentrate on one thing at a time while thinking how what we're doing now affects what we need to do later, making later tasks easier.

7. Create Objects

Again, the list we've created should make it easier. A little thought can make my list more complete, and then I can create everything, marking them off on the list as I go.

8. Add Resets

Having created all the mobs and objects, we can now create resets for them to load around the area. Reading the resets section of the OLCGuide is a really good idea before starting, as is developing a plan of how to work through them systematically. Some people will do all the resets in one room first, whereas others will create resets for all mobs in order and then all objects. Just ensure you have a plan that will mean you don't forget to add resets where you wanted them.

9. Add Progs

Good programs can really make an area feel alive. I tend to add progs after I've done all other resets, so I can see all my mobs around the area and see where some action is needed. If I've created a miniquest or two within the area, I'll need progs to make them work. However, it adds a lot of character to an area to extend progs beyond what's absolutely essential for your area to function. It also means that the progs for your miniquests are not immediately obvious, so people have
to work harder and explore your area more closely to complete them.

10. Add Finishing Touches

It'd be easy to "finish" the area now, but now is also the time you can add more texture and fun parts. It's better to spend some time adding them now than having the area implemented and thinking later how much you wish you'd included them. You might have thought of a few additional mobs, objects or progs to enhance the area. There might be extra descs that you want to add. There are the weather/time updates and credits to think of.

Getting another Builder to explore your area might also help you to identify areas where you could further improve it.

11. Check Everything

Check through your area thoroughly. Although someone else will check it before it's implemented, it helps a lot for you to go through as well as you can rather than leaving the work to them. Check the spelling in your room descriptions. Make sure your mobiles and equipment meet the requirements of the points systems. Read through your notes and ensure there's nothing you meant to include that you forgot. If your area is for a particular level range, create another character of the
appropriate level and try the area out yourself. With the Area Immortals' permission, get a friend to help you test and check- they might be able to give you a good impartial view before the area is implemented.

I'm sure people can think of improvements on this, and other tips that may be useful. Feel free to note them to the Immortal Staff so that we can put them up so that others might benefit from them. I just hope that this provides a starting point from which people can make their own strategy and thus succeed in producing great areas that players will find interesting and entertaining, and that thus provide a source of pride for the Builder.

Good luck.
