5)   Describing the room

Among one of the most difficult tasks of building, alongside planning the area, is writing room descriptions. To help you, here are a list of tips and questions you should ask yourself when writing the descriptions:

General tips

  • Try to avoid describing what the weather is like at the moment, since weather updates do that. Feel free to say, from the state of the landscape, what the weather is usually like, or what the temperature is like, however.
  • Sight is not the only sense. While it is important to describe what people can see, they also notice things using other senses. On a beach, for example, they'd most likely noticed the gulls flying overhead by the noise they make. They cannot see the sea air, but they can certainly smell it. Describe sounds and smells rather than just saying 'it smells awful'.

Grammar and language

  • If possible, avoid using the word "you" in the room description, as this is often viewed as amateurish.
  • Please try to avoid forced RP, so don't write “as you travel west along the road...” if someone could equally be going east. Generally, “you feel” is bad. The classic example on prophecy would be at a site of Grolim sacrifice, writing 'you feel sickened by the sight'. Yes, that's true for most people, but a Grolim might be in a state of religious ecstasy, for example.
  • Use proper English in your descriptions. Slang words won't be recognisable by everyone.
  • Finish your sentences. If I’ve made the effort to read a description, it's annoying for it to...
  • Be consistent. "northeast" "north east" "north-east" are all acceptable, but be consistent between different descriptions. Reread your descriptions aloud to see if they make sense. Often, this is also the best way to check the spelling, punctuation and grammar.

Formatting

  • Room descriptions should be five lines or more, or else the area will look very bare. Obviously a room description too long means it will be a nightmare to walk through.
  • Try not to use too much colour in the room description - imagine speedwalking through a colourful area with brief off! Colour only the important words in the room description, for example, if you have a tree growing next to a bank, the bank is more important than the tree, and so colour the word "bank" and not "tree".
  • Always format your room description by typing ".f"
  • Add colour to the description after formatting the room description.

Repeating room descriptions

  • Repeating five or so room descriptions when describing similar rooms (e.g. roads, forests, wilderness) are fine, but repeating fifty is too much
  • Try varying each room a little. For example if there is a house next to the road, you can add "To the east stands a magnificent house."

Room descriptions

City/countryside roads

  • Describe the road, path, alley etc. (Are they wide? Clean? What are they made out of?)
  • What is around the road? (Trees? Bushes? Only buildings?)
  • What kinds of buildings are around the road? (Are they grand? Old? Falling apart?)
  • What kind of scenery? (Mountains in the distance? Tall towers/buildings nearby?)
  • Describe the people walking on the roads (How many? What kind? Merchants? Children? Generally moving in what direction?)

Indoor rooms

  • What is in the room? (Furniture, decorations, lighting, fireplaces, carpets etc.)
  • Describe the walls (Any tapestries? Paintings? Murals? What is the wall made out of?)
  • Describe the exits (Doors? Trapdoors? Windows? Stairs?)

Countryside

  • What kind of landscape is this? (Forest, plains, deserts, shrub land, jungle, swamp, mountains)

    Forest: Dense or sparse (number of trees), type of tree (mainly oaks? Mixed? Conifers? Deciduous?), if sparse may be able to see what is two or more rooms away, if dense may be dark
    Plains: Vegetation (how tall is grass? Colour of flowers if any), hilly or not? If not hilly can see far away objects, e.g. mountains, forests, but not in centre of Algar plains.
    Deserts: Vegetation (none? Cacti? Tumbleweed? A few tufts of grass?), tall or short dunes or flat sand? If flat/short dunes, can see far away objects (not in deep deserts though)
    Mountains: Altitude (high up, near foot of mountain) will affect vegetation and climate. Slope? If steep less vegetation. Rivers/streams (at the top generally ice or boggy land, streams, then becomes river winding through narrow valleys, valley widens as approaches bottom of the mountains, and floodplains). High up: snow? Glaciers? Ice? If glaciers, wide valleys, jagged walls, else narrow valleys.

Coastal/sea/river:

  • Beaches: Sandy/rocky/pebble beach? Colour of sand (white, yellow, grey/black is volcanic)? Vegetation (marrow grass)? Narrow or wide beach? Cliffs (steep or gentle, colour or rock type)? Wave strength (if bay calm, if windy area strong waves)?
  • Sea: If in sea, currents (warm? Cold? If strong can add mprogs to transfer players), depth, clarity of water, colour of water (blue or green?), vegetation (corals in warmer, shallow waters, kelp grows in cool, shallow waters), light (more closer to the surface, less deeper down we go)
  • River: Rivers are separated into three sections - upper, middle and lower course. Upper - narrow, deep valley, rapids, waterfalls, clear, fast moving water. Middle - winding through mountains, slightly murky near bottom of river, wider valley, water moves relatively fast. Lower - slow, murky, silty water, terraces, ox-bow lakes (U-shaped lakes), winds through floodplain.
